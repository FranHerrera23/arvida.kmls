02 — A Shared Language

We light in a grammar you already read.

The person who'll live here thinks in frames — the controlled dark of Kubrick, the low glow of Lynch. Light that withholds as much as it reveals. That isn't a mood board for us; it's how we already work: single intentional sources, gallery-grade restraint, deliberate shadow.

The studio that knows when not to add light is the one that understands it.

Reading Arvida — 01

Material first.

Plaster, iroko, microcement, felt — surfaces chosen for texture, not shine. They don't reflect light; they hold it. Our work is to let each material reveal what it already is.

Light as a way of seeing the craft — not hiding it.
Reading Arvida — 02

The screening room is the heart, not an amenity.

In most homes the cinema is a feature. Here it's the center of gravity — the room a film lover actually lives toward. So we treat the whole house with a director's logic: light built in scenes, not switches. Pools of warmth and deliberate dark. The dome above the stair that stays dark on purpose, so the glow below feels warmer. A home where atmosphere is composed, the way a scene is — and where, when the lights go down, nothing pulls your eye away from the screen.

Lit the way a film is graded — for feeling, not for brightness.
03 — Our Craft

How we light
what you're building

Four materials, four decisions. This is the difference between a lighting plan and a room you don't want to leave.
Plaster

Curved, hand-finished, no shine to hide behind. Grazing light only — a low raking wash along the curve, so the spiral stair reads as texture, not surface. The dome above stays dark on purpose: a pool of shadow that makes the warmth below feel warmer.

The quiet drama of the stair.
Wood — iroko & teak

Warm, directional, full of grain. Tight accents raked across the figure, color temperature held golden. Never flooded — the grain reads as depth, not gloss.

Lit from within, not spotlit from above.
Microcement

Soft, matte, seamless — almost no reflectance. One well-placed source, grazing or concealed, over many. Restraint keeps the surface continuous.

Less light, better placed.
Acoustic felt

Absorbs sound and light alike. Indirect, warm, layered low — never aimed at the panels. The surface stays matte, the room closes in around you.

Light you sit inside, not under.

06 — Scope of Work

What we deliver,
stage by stage.

Three contractual stages, in coordination with Studio Valle de Valle. No ambiguity — each stage is a defined set of deliverables.
Stage 1

Lighting Design

The complete design intent for every renovated space — concept, fixtures, and the technical basis behind each decision.

Deliverables

  • Lighting concept & atmosphere, per space
  • Reflected ceiling plans (RCP) — fixture locations & types
  • Fixture schedule & specifications
  • Photometric calculations stage to confirm
  • Control-system strategy — scenes, zoning, dimming system to confirm
  • Full construction dossier
Stage 2

Lighting Design Management

We protect the design through construction — coordinating with your team and resolving every change before it reaches the wall.

Deliverables

  • Coordination with architect, interior designer, AV & structural consultants
  • Shop-drawing & submittal review
  • Change management through construction
  • Scene-calibration planning
  • Delivery in your file standards formats to confirm
Stage 3

On-Site Management

Site visits, supervision and final commissioning — we don't leave until the light is exactly right.

Deliverables

  • Site visits & installation supervision
  • Aiming, focusing & light testing
  • Scene programming & commissioning system to confirm
  • Final walkthrough & punch list
Optional · Rendered Lighting Simulation — photorealistic visualization of representative spaces, before a single fixture is installed.

09 — The Studio
For 33 years, Karen Mannheim Lighting Studio has done one thing: make light give a space its meaning.

What began as one person knocking on doors in Lima is now a practice of more than 80 people across three continents, with offices in Lima, Miami and Madrid. For a Coral Gables home, our Miami studio is already next door — not flying in, but here.

We've delivered more than 2,500 projects across six countries, working alongside firms like RAMSA, Oppenheim and Adriana Hoyos. But the number that matters most isn't on this list: it's that after three decades and that much scale, the studio still designs each project as if it were the only one. The throughline never changed — light, here, is never decoration. It's the soul of the architecture.
